Usability

The usability of quick response (QR) codes. QR codes are a very common appearance but are they fit-for-purpose or well used? Does the use of QR code functionality make a mobile application more usable? Usability tests identify areas where people struggle with a product and help you make recommendations for improvements for the applications. The goal is to better understand how real users interact with QR code and to improve interaction between users and the QR code. Since "usability testing employs real users accomplishing real tasks, it can provide objective performance data" (TechSmith, 2017) and allow observations on the users to help improve and enhance the interaction between the users and how to use the QR code.

Using open-ended and reasonably context-free questions allow us to gather data from interviewees without any interference or influence such as unintentionally pushing interviewees towards an answer we want or interviewees seeing the interview as a 'test', changing their answers to score well.
This also allowed us to gather more information with an infinite set of possible answers. Open-ended questions are more suitable in relation to the usability of QR codes however, requires more effort to analyse the data. (Open Ended Questions Article We Researched).

Adoption

In order for an interaction technique to be successfully adopted by a wide range of people, three conditions must be met, (Cheng, Y. and Budiu, R.):

  • Usefulness: The technique must provide some sort of benefit. Useless innovations are a dead-end.
  • Ease of use: This technique has to provide some sort of benefit in terms of the learning curve and the usability of the technique once it is learnt. If the learning curve is too great or mastery of the technique requires time/effort, most will give up.
  • Discoverability: The technique/technology must be discoverable by a wide population. Many useful inventions have simply been lost because of the lack of numbers to support such techniques.

Why we chose Quick Response Codes

The reason that we as a team chose to research into QR codes is that we feel that they can utilize a powerful functionality through vision to convey meaningful information. However, the way in which they are accessed through an application is pivotal to the success of the QR feature. They have seen very common use in Asia, "QR Codes are part of daily life in Japan, Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong and China" says (Rouillard, 2008). As well as the interesting truth of what (Chang, Jung & Shin, 2012) discussed around how one of the main weaknesses of QR codes is their limited interactivity. As it is required to have access to a smartphone to be able to consistently use QR codes they are unusable to a vast amount of the population. The codes's themselves don't speak to consumers and the consumers have to unveil the curtain, so to speak, to access the information that the code contains. This fact got us interested in how people where using QR Codes and whether they found that they used them commonly within mobile applications.

Experiment content_paste

#1 Interview

The goal of the interview is to get an understanding of how different people would respond to a set of interview questions around QR Codes.
We planned with these questions to get a general understanding of whether our sample group knew about and how to use QR Code and in for what function they would use them for.

Questions:

  • Do you know what this is? (Shows image of QR code)
  • How do you think it looks?
  • How would you use this?
  • Are you a regular user of apps on your phone?
  • What are your most-used apps?
  • Do you use QR codes in any mobile application?
  • If so, what? And for what purpose?

#2 Usability (Test) Speed

After conducting some interviews we thought it would be useful to get users to perform a simple task in two applications that use QR Codes.
So we asked users to find the QR function contained within Snapchat and Messenger to add new friends.

  • First the interviewee would be given Andre's iPhone.
  • Ask them to navigate to the snapchat and messenger applications and to stay within each app for between 30 seconds and 1 minute.
  • Ask them to navigate to the home screen of the iphone
  • Then hand them this document.
  • Observations would be to see firstly which app they chose to enter first.
  • Then to capture the time it takes to find the screen from launching each application.
  • As well as capturing the number of screen taps within each app to reach the desired screen.

Our second experiment revealed how it was more common for users to know how to navigate Snapchat in comparison to Facebook Messenger. It seemed that of our 3 participants 2 where accustomed to Android devices an felt that the application behaved differently on the Apple Device. We found that people generally favour Snapchat 'Stories' over Messenger/Facebook stories. Many people from both this and the first experiment had used QR codes within Snapchat to add friends but had never used them to do the same in Messenger. This we believe validates our many goal that QR codes can have highly usable functionality within mobile applications. But, it depends heavily on how intuitive it is to find and use, as well as whether the users of the application want to use a QR code to complete that function.
